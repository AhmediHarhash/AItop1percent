# Chapter 7 — The Latency-Cost-Quality Triangle

Every real-time AI system lives inside a triangle with three vertices: latency, cost, and quality. You can optimize for any two. You cannot optimize for all three. Faster responses require either more expensive infrastructure or simpler models that produce lower quality. Higher quality requires either larger models that cost more or longer processing times that increase latency. Lower cost requires either slower responses or cheaper models that sacrifice output quality. This is not a problem to solve. It is a constraint to manage.

The teams that handle this tradeoff well do not pretend the constraint does not exist. They make it explicit. They set latency budgets with dollar signs attached, knowing that shaving fifty milliseconds off a response might double the compute bill. They use streaming and partial responses to create the perception of speed without paying for actual speed. They separate user-facing flows, where latency tolerance is measured in hundreds of milliseconds, from background processing, where latency tolerance is measured in minutes and cost drops dramatically. They build geographic routing strategies that balance data residency requirements with latency targets and infrastructure costs. And they design SLAs that reflect the economic reality of the triangle rather than pretending all three corners can be maximized simultaneously.

This chapter maps every edge of the triangle. The math behind latency budgets and their cost implications. Streaming architectures that trade actual quality for perceived quality. Async processing patterns that trade latency for dramatic savings. Timeout and retry economics that reveal hidden costs in reliability mechanisms. And the design of latency SLAs that account for what each millisecond actually costs.
