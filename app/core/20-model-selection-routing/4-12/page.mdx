# 4.12 — Negotiating Enterprise Agreements: Volume Discounts, Committed Use, and Reserved Capacity

In November 2025, a financial services company signed a one-year committed use agreement with their primary AI provider, committing to $1.2 million in annual spend in exchange for a 25% discount off list pricing. They based the commitment on their current $80,000 monthly run rate, projecting growth to $100,000 monthly by mid-year. By February 2026, three things happened that invalidated their assumptions. First, a competitor launched a product that cut their user growth by 40%. Second, they successfully migrated 60% of their traffic to a cheaper model, cutting costs per request by half. Third, the provider announced a 35% price cut on the model they were using, which meant list pricing dropped below their contracted rate. They were locked into spending $1.2 million when their actual usage would cost $620,000 at the new list prices. They had no clause to renegotiate mid-contract. They either had to spend $580,000 on capacity they did not need or forfeit the unused commitment and pay list price on their actual usage, losing the discount entirely. The finance team escalated it to the CFO. The engineering team scrambled to find ways to burn through the commitment by expanding AI usage into marginal use cases that did not justify the cost. The root cause was not bad faith from the provider. It was signing a committed use agreement without understanding the risks, without contractual flexibility, and without stress-testing the commitment against downside scenarios. They saved 25% on the deal they thought they were getting and lost 40% on the deal they actually got.

Enterprise agreements with AI providers can cut your costs by 20-50% compared to pay-as-you-go pricing. They can also lock you into spending money you do not need, trap you with a single provider when better options emerge, and saddle you with contract terms that seem reasonable when you sign but become punitive when your usage patterns change. This subchapter shows you how enterprise pricing works, what providers actually negotiate on, how to prepare for a negotiation, which contract terms to watch for, and when enterprise agreements make sense versus staying on flexible pay-as-you-go pricing.

## How Enterprise Pricing Works with AI Providers

AI providers operate on tiered pricing models. Pay-as-you-go is the default: you pay list price per token, per image, per audio minute, with no commitment and no negotiation. This is the published rate card. It works for small-scale experimentation and for teams spending less than $10,000 monthly. Once you cross $10,000 monthly, you enter the tier where volume discounts become available. Once you cross $50,000 monthly, you enter the tier where committed use agreements and reserved capacity become negotiable. The exact thresholds vary by provider, but the structure is universal: low-volume users pay list price, high-volume users negotiate custom rates.

**Volume discounts** are the simplest form of enterprise pricing. You commit to a minimum monthly spend, and the provider gives you a percentage discount off list pricing. For example, commit to $50,000 monthly and receive 15% off. Commit to $150,000 monthly and receive 25% off. Commit to $500,000 monthly and receive 35% off. The discount applies to all usage up to your commitment. If you commit to $50,000 and spend $60,000, you get the discount on the first $50,000 and pay list price on the incremental $10,000. If you commit to $50,000 and spend only $40,000, you still pay $50,000—you are billed for the full commitment regardless of actual usage. This is the fundamental trade-off: you lock in a lower rate in exchange for a minimum spend guarantee.

Volume discounts do not require annual contracts. You can negotiate month-to-month committed spend with a 30-day or 90-day termination notice. This gives you flexibility to adjust your commitment as usage changes. Some providers require quarterly or annual commits for higher discount tiers. The longer the commitment period, the higher the discount, because the provider values revenue predictability. A one-year commit at $100,000 monthly might earn you 28% off, while a month-to-month commit at the same spend level earns you 18% off. The 10-point discount delta is the premium you pay for flexibility.

**Committed use agreements** are multi-month or multi-year contracts where you commit to a total spend amount over the contract term in exchange for a discount. For example, commit to $1.5 million over 12 months and receive 30% off list pricing. You can spend the commitment however you like across the term—$200,000 one month, $80,000 the next—as long as you hit the total by the end of the contract. Some agreements require minimum monthly spend thresholds to prevent you from front-loading or back-loading usage. Read the fine print. If the contract says you must spend at least 50% of your monthly proration each month, you cannot defer spend to later quarters.

Committed use agreements often include true-up provisions. At the end of the contract term, if you spent less than your commitment, you owe the difference. If you committed to $1.5 million and spent $1.2 million, you owe $300,000 for capacity you did not use. Some providers allow you to roll over unused commitment to the next contract term, but this is not standard. Negotiate rollover provisions upfront if you want them. If you spent more than your commitment, the overage is typically billed at list price or at a reduced discount tier. If you committed to $1.5 million with a 30% discount and spent $1.8 million, you pay $1.05 million for the committed portion and $300,000 at list price for the overage, unless you negotiated overage discount language.

**Reserved capacity** is an emerging pricing model where you pay upfront for guaranteed throughput. Instead of committing to dollar spend, you commit to requests per minute or tokens per minute at a specific model for a fixed monthly or annual fee. For example, pay $40,000 monthly for guaranteed 500 requests per minute on GPT-5 with no per-token charges. If your average usage is 300 requests per minute, you are paying for unused capacity. If your peak usage hits 600 requests per minute, you exceed your reservation and pay overage fees. Reserved capacity makes sense for workloads with predictable, steady traffic where you want cost certainty and protection from rate limits. It does not make sense for bursty or unpredictable workloads where you would pay for idle capacity most of the time.

Reserved capacity contracts often include latency guarantees and priority access. You are not just buying throughput; you are buying service level commitments. During periods of high demand when pay-as-you-go users experience throttling or elevated latency, your reserved capacity insulates you. This is valuable for production systems where latency spikes break user experience. The premium you pay for reserved capacity is insurance against provider congestion.

The newest pricing model, emerging in early 2026, is **capacity-as-a-service**, where you lease dedicated inference infrastructure from the provider. Instead of sharing a multi-tenant pool, you get isolated GPUs allocated exclusively to your workload. Pricing is typically a flat monthly fee based on GPU count and model size, not per-token usage. This model appeals to enterprises with strict data residency, compliance, or performance isolation requirements. It is the most expensive option on a per-token basis but offers the highest level of control and predictability. Most teams never need it. Consider it only if you are spending $500,000 monthly and have non-negotiable isolation requirements.

## What Providers Actually Negotiate On

Providers publish rate cards, but everything is negotiable at enterprise scale. The question is not whether you can negotiate. The question is what leverage you have and what terms the provider cares about. Understanding what providers actually negotiate on helps you structure a deal that benefits both sides.

Providers negotiate on **discount percentage** first. This is the most visible term and the one you will spend the most time discussing. List price for GPT-5 input tokens is $0.000015. You want $0.000010. That is a 33% discount. The provider counters at 20%. You settle at 28%. This is table stakes. Every enterprise negotiation involves haggling over the discount. Your leverage here is volume, growth trajectory, and competitive alternatives. If you are currently spending $100,000 monthly and projecting to $300,000 within a year, you have growth leverage. If you are running a competitive bake-off between three providers and credibly threaten to move your traffic, you have competitive leverage.

Providers negotiate on **commitment amount and duration**. You want a low commitment to preserve flexibility. The provider wants a high commitment to lock in revenue. Meet in the middle by structuring tiered commitments with step-ups. Commit to $60,000 monthly for the first quarter, $80,000 for the second quarter, and $100,000 for the second half of the year, with the option to increase further based on usage. This gives the provider revenue growth visibility and gives you flexibility to ramp without overcommitting early. Providers generally accept step-up commitments if they see a credible growth plan backed by actual usage trends.

Providers negotiate on **rollover and true-up terms**. Standard contracts have no rollover: unused commitment is forfeited. Push for rollover provisions that let you carry unused commitment into the next quarter or the next contract term. Providers resist this because it delays revenue recognition, but they accept it if you are a large, strategic customer or if you commit to a longer contract duration in exchange. For example, commit to 18 months instead of 12 in exchange for quarterly rollover rights. The provider gets revenue certainty; you get flexibility.

Providers negotiate on **overage pricing**. Standard contracts bill overages at list price, which eliminates your discount on incremental usage. Negotiate overage discount tiers. If your commitment is $100,000 monthly at 25% off and you spend $120,000, negotiate that the $20,000 overage is billed at 15% off instead of list price. Some providers agree to extend your full discount to overages if your overage is less than 20% of your commitment. Others have hard policies against discounting overages. This is worth pushing on because overage fees compound quickly if your usage spikes.

Providers negotiate on **model coverage**. Some committed use agreements lock you to a specific model: GPT-5, Claude Opus 4.5, Gemini 3 Pro. If the provider releases a new model mid-contract, you have to pay list price for the new model or renegotiate the contract. Negotiate model family coverage instead of single-model coverage. Your commitment covers all models in the GPT-5 family, including GPT-5-mini, GPT-5, and GPT-5-turbo. This gives you flexibility to optimize costs by shifting traffic to cheaper models within the family without losing your discount. Providers resist family coverage if the price differential between models is large, but they accept it if you commit to higher volume.

Providers negotiate on **early termination terms**. Standard contracts have no early termination: you are locked in for the full term, and breaking the contract means you forfeit the discount and owe the full list price for all usage during the contract. Push for termination clauses with reasonable notice and penalties. For example, you can terminate with 90 days notice and a 10% early termination fee on the remaining commitment. Providers accept termination clauses if you are a high-growth customer they want to keep happy or if the contract duration is long enough that early termination is unlikely.

Providers negotiate on **service level agreements**. Pay-as-you-go pricing includes no SLA. Enterprise agreements can include uptime guarantees, latency guarantees, and rate limit guarantees. For example, 99.9% uptime, P95 latency under 800 milliseconds, and guaranteed 200 requests per minute with no throttling. If the provider misses the SLA, you receive service credits—typically 5-10% of your monthly spend for each SLA violation. SLAs cost the provider nothing if they deliver, so they are often willing to include them to close the deal. Push for SLAs if your application is latency-sensitive or if you have experienced reliability issues on pay-as-you-go.

Providers do not negotiate on published pricing for small customers. If you are spending $5,000 monthly, you pay list price. If you are spending $80,000 monthly and growing, everything is negotiable. The key is to approach the negotiation with data: your current spend, your growth trajectory, your cost model projections, and a credible competitive alternative. Providers negotiate with customers who can walk. If you cannot walk, you have no leverage.

## Preparing for an Enterprise Agreement Negotiation

Negotiations succeed or fail before you ever talk to the provider. Preparation is everything. You need three things before you start: usage data, growth projections, and a competitive alternative. Without these, you are negotiating from weakness.

Start with **usage data**. Export the last six months of usage from your provider dashboard or billing API. You need monthly spend, request count, token count by model, and cost per request. Calculate your growth rate month over month. If you went from $40,000 in August to $95,000 in January, your six-month growth rate is 138%. Providers care about growth because it signals future revenue. A flat $100,000 monthly customer is less attractive than a $100,000 customer growing 30% quarterly. Show the growth trend in a simple chart: monthly spend over time with a trendline. This is your credibility.

Build **growth projections** using your cost model. Show three scenarios: conservative, expected, and aggressive. Conservative projects 20% growth over the next year, expected projects 50%, aggressive projects 100%. Tie each scenario to product milestones: new feature launches, new customer segments, new use cases. Providers do not believe hockey-stick projections without a narrative. The narrative is: we are launching feature X in Q2, which we expect to add 15,000 new users and increase requests per user by 30%. We are expanding into Europe in Q3, which we expect to double our user base. We are integrating AI into our mobile app in Q4, which we expect to increase total requests by 40%. Each milestone is a line item with a projected cost impact. This is how you justify your growth projections.

Identify a **competitive alternative**. Run a bake-off with at least two other providers. Measure quality, latency, and cost for your key use cases. If Provider A costs $0.000015 per token and Provider B costs $0.000010 per token with equivalent quality, you have leverage to negotiate Provider A down or credibly threaten to switch. The bake-off does not have to be complete. It just has to be real. Providers know when you are bluffing. If you walk into a negotiation and say you are considering switching but have no data, the provider will not take you seriously. If you walk in and say you ran a two-week production test with Provider B, achieved equivalent quality at 35% lower cost, and are preparing to migrate unless Provider A matches the price, you have leverage.

Prepare a **target deal structure** before the first call. Write down your ideal outcome: discount percentage, commitment amount, contract duration, rollover terms, overage pricing, model coverage, SLA terms. Write down your walk-away threshold: if the provider will not go below this discount or will not agree to these terms, you walk. Have a clear ask. Do not go into the negotiation hoping the provider will propose favorable terms. They will propose the least favorable terms you will accept. You propose the most favorable terms they will accept. You meet in the middle.

Assign a **lead negotiator**. This is usually a senior engineer, a finance lead, or a procurement specialist, depending on your organization. The lead negotiator owns the relationship with the provider account team and coordinates internally with engineering, finance, and legal. Do not negotiate by committee. The provider does not want to talk to seven people with conflicting priorities. They want one point of contact who can make decisions.

Schedule the **first call with your account manager**, not with a generic sales team. If you do not have an account manager, request one. Once you cross $50,000 monthly spend, you should have a named account manager and access to solution architects. Use them. The account manager is your internal advocate at the provider. They want to keep you happy because their comp is tied to your retention and growth. Be direct: we are spending $95,000 monthly, projecting to $180,000 by year-end, and we want to negotiate a committed use agreement with a 30% discount. What is possible?

The account manager will likely say they need to check with their pricing team. That is fine. Give them a week. They will come back with a counter-proposal. This is the start of the negotiation. Do not accept the first offer. Counter with something more aggressive. If they offer 20% off for a $1.2 million annual commit, counter with 30% off for a $1 million commit with quarterly rollover. They will counter again. You will iterate three to five times. Most deals close within two weeks of the first call if both sides are motivated.

## The Enterprise Agreement Pitfalls: What to Watch For

Enterprise agreements save money if your usage matches your projections. They destroy value if your usage falls short, if you overcommit, or if you lock into terms that become unfavorable when conditions change. These are the pitfalls that catch most teams.

**Overcommitting on volume** is the most common mistake. You commit to $1.5 million annually based on aggressive growth projections. Growth does not materialize. You spend $900,000. You owe $600,000 in true-up fees for capacity you did not use. You effectively paid 67% more per token than you would have on pay-as-you-go. The fix is to commit conservatively. Use the low end of your growth projection range, not the high end. Better to exceed your commitment and pay list price on the overage than to undershoot and forfeit money.

**Underestimating growth** is the opposite mistake. You commit to $800,000 annually to secure a 25% discount. You end up spending $1.6 million. The first $800,000 gets the discount, but the incremental $800,000 is billed at list price. Your blended discount is only 12.5%. If you had committed to $1.6 million upfront, you might have secured 32% off. You left money on the table. The fix is to build in expansion options. Negotiate that if you exceed your commitment by more than 20%, you can renegotiate the contract mid-term to extend the discount to all usage. Providers sometimes agree to this because it locks in incremental revenue at a predictable margin.

**Locking into a single provider** eliminates your ability to optimize cost by model later. You commit 100% of your volume to Provider A. Six months later, Provider B releases a model that delivers equivalent quality at 40% lower cost. You cannot switch without breaking your contract and forfeiting your discount. The fix is to commit to multi-provider agreements or to commit only 70-80% of your volume, leaving 20-30% flexible for experimentation and competitive pressure. Some teams negotiate contingency clauses: if a competitor offers better pricing or quality, you have the right to reduce your commitment by 25% with 60 days notice. Providers hate these clauses, but they accept them if you are a strategic customer.

**Ignoring price change clauses** leaves you vulnerable to mid-contract price increases. Some contracts include language that the provider can adjust pricing if underlying compute costs increase or if they release new models with different pricing structures. If you signed a committed use agreement at $0.000015 per token and the provider raises list pricing to $0.000020 mid-contract, does your discount apply to the old price or the new price? If the contract says the discount applies to "then-current list pricing," your rate just went up 33%. Negotiate that your contracted rate is fixed in absolute dollar terms, not as a discount percentage off a variable list price. Lock in $0.000010 per token for the contract term, regardless of what happens to list pricing.

**Failing to account for model lifecycle** creates cost risk. You commit to using GPT-5 for two years. One year in, the provider releases GPT-6 with 2x better quality at the same price. You are stuck on GPT-5 under contract. The rest of the market moves to GPT-6. Your product quality falls behind. The fix is to negotiate model upgrade rights. If the provider releases a newer model in the same family, you have the right to shift your commitment to the new model at the same rate. Providers sometimes include this automatically in family-level agreements, but not always. Ask explicitly.

**Ignoring audit rights** exposes you to billing disputes. Your contract should include the right to audit your usage data and billing calculations. Providers occasionally have billing bugs that overcharge customers. If you have no audit rights, you have no recourse. Negotiate that you can request detailed usage logs and billing breakdowns at any time and that the provider will correct billing errors within 30 days. This is standard in enterprise software contracts and should be standard in AI contracts.

**Skipping legal review** leads to unfavorable liability and indemnification terms. Some AI provider contracts include broad limitations of liability, no indemnification for IP infringement claims, and clauses that put all risk on you if the model outputs cause harm. If your use case is high-risk—healthcare, finance, legal—these terms are unacceptable. Have your legal team review the contract before you sign. Negotiate indemnification for IP claims, negotiate reasonable liability caps, and negotiate insurance requirements if necessary. Providers push back, but they negotiate if you are spending enough.

Every pitfall is avoidable with careful contract drafting and realistic projections. Do not optimize for the maximum discount. Optimize for the best risk-adjusted outcome. A 28% discount with rollover, overage protection, and early termination rights is better than a 35% discount with a hard commitment, no rollover, and punitive overage fees.

## When Enterprise Agreements Make Sense vs Pay-As-You-Go

Enterprise agreements are not always the right answer. They make sense under specific conditions. Outside those conditions, you are better off on flexible pay-as-you-go pricing, even if it costs more per token.

Enterprise agreements make sense when **usage is predictable and growing**. If you have been spending $80,000 to $120,000 monthly for six consecutive months and your product roadmap shows continued growth, you have enough data to commit with confidence. Lock in a discount. If your usage swings wildly—$30,000 one month, $150,000 the next—you cannot confidently commit to a floor without overpaying in low months.

Enterprise agreements make sense when **you are optimizing for cost at scale**. If you are spending $200,000 monthly, a 25% discount saves $50,000 monthly or $600,000 annually. That is real money that funds two engineers or three product managers. The cost of negotiating and managing the contract is negligible compared to the savings. If you are spending $8,000 monthly, a 25% discount saves $2,000 monthly or $24,000 annually. That is meaningful, but probably not worth the overhead of contract negotiation, quarterly true-ups, and finance reporting.

Enterprise agreements make sense when **you need latency or throughput guarantees**. If you are running a production system where rate limits or latency spikes break user experience, reserved capacity with SLAs is worth paying for. If you are running batch workloads where latency does not matter and you can tolerate occasional throttling, pay-as-you-go with retry logic is cheaper.

Enterprise agreements make sense when **provider pricing is stable**. If your provider has not changed pricing in two years and the market is mature, locking in a rate for 12 months is low-risk. If the provider has cut prices 40% twice in the last year and new competitors are entering the market with aggressive pricing, locking in a rate is risky. You might commit to $0.000010 per token and watch list pricing drop to $0.000006 six months later. Stay flexible.

Pay-as-you-go makes sense when **you are still experimenting**. If you are testing multiple models, tuning prompts, and figuring out which use cases are viable, you need flexibility. Committing to a specific model or a minimum spend constrains your ability to pivot. Pay the premium for flexibility until your architecture stabilizes.

Pay-as-you-go makes sense when **your usage is declining**. If you launched an AI feature, saw weak adoption, and are spending less each month, do not commit to maintaining spend levels. Let it drop. Reevaluate after six months. If usage rebounds, negotiate then.

Pay-as-you-go makes sense when **you are multi-provider by design**. If you are running a routing layer that dynamically allocates traffic across three providers based on cost, quality, and latency, you do not want to commit 80% of your volume to one provider. Committing defeats the purpose of multi-provider flexibility. Instead, negotiate smaller commitments with each provider—maybe 30% to each—so you maintain the ability to shift traffic based on real-time conditions.

The decision is not binary. You can run hybrid pricing: commit 60% of your volume to lock in a discount, keep 40% on pay-as-you-go for flexibility. You can commit to quarterly terms instead of annual terms, giving you more frequent decision points. You can start with pay-as-you-go, monitor usage for six months, and then lock in a commitment once patterns stabilize. There is no one-size-fits-all answer. The right choice depends on your usage patterns, growth trajectory, risk tolerance, and the market dynamics of your providers.

What is non-negotiable is that you make the decision deliberately, with data, and with a clear understanding of the trade-offs. Do not sign an enterprise agreement because your account manager offered a discount and it sounded good. Do not stay on pay-as-you-go because negotiating feels hard. Build the cost model, run the scenarios, calculate the savings, assess the risks, and then decide. That is the discipline that separates teams that manage AI costs effectively from teams that overpay by 40% because they never asked the question.
