# 30.3.8 — Choosing Your Charge Metric: The Decision That Shapes Everything

The **Metric Mismatch Anti-Pattern** is the most expensive pricing mistake in AI, and it looks perfectly reasonable from the inside.

A team builds an AI document analysis product. The product reads contracts, extracts key terms, flags risks, and generates summaries. The team needs to choose what to bill for. They look at their infrastructure: every analysis consumes tokens — input tokens for the document, output tokens for the analysis. Tokens are easy to meter. The billing system already tracks them. The engineering team can implement token-based pricing in a week. So they price per token.

Six months later, sales velocity has stalled. Enterprise buyers do not understand tokens. They do not know how many tokens a contract contains. They cannot predict whether a fifty-page contract will consume 40,000 tokens or 120,000 tokens. Every procurement conversation stalls at the same question: "What will this cost us per month?" The sales team cannot answer because the answer depends on document length, complexity, and formatting — variables the customer cannot estimate and the vendor cannot predict without analyzing the actual documents. A competitor launches with per-document pricing. Customers immediately understand it: $3 per analyzed contract. No token math. No unpredictability. The competitor captures the market while the first company debates internally whether tokens were ever the right metric.

This team did not choose the wrong price. They chose the wrong unit. The **charge metric** — the fundamental unit of billing — is the most consequential pricing decision you will make because it shapes four things simultaneously: how customers think about your cost, how your sales team sells, how your product competes, and how your margin structure behaves.

## What a Charge Metric Is and Why It Matters

Your charge metric is the unit that appears on the invoice. It is the atom of your pricing model. Everything else — the platform fee, the tiers, the overage rates, the discounts — is built on top of it. Choose per-token, and your entire commercial architecture revolves around token economics. Choose per-document, and it revolves around document economics. Choose per-resolution, and it revolves around outcome economics. The choice is not reversible without significant customer friction, sales retraining, and contract renegotiation.

The charge metric matters because it is the unit the customer uses to calculate ROI. When a customer evaluates whether to buy your product, they perform a mental calculation — often literally a spreadsheet calculation — that compares your price against their current cost for the same unit of work. If your charge metric matches the unit the customer already thinks in, the calculation is instant. A customer support leader thinks in tickets resolved. A legal operations manager thinks in contracts reviewed. A sales director thinks in meetings booked. If your charge metric is "resolutions," "contracts analyzed," or "meetings booked," the customer can compute ROI in their head: "We pay $12 per ticket with human agents, this costs $0.99 per resolution, the savings are obvious."

If your charge metric does not match, the customer has to translate. "We pay $12 per ticket, but this product charges per token, and a typical ticket uses 4,000 input tokens and 800 output tokens, and the rate is... hold on, let me build a spreadsheet." Translation introduces friction. Friction slows deals. Slow deals lose to competitors with simpler math. The charge metric is not a billing detail. It is a sales velocity lever.

## The Charge Metric Spectrum

Bessemer Venture Partners published a framework in their AI pricing and monetization playbook that organizes charge metrics along a spectrum from infrastructure to outcome. This spectrum is the most useful lens for evaluating charge metric options because it maps each metric to a set of trade-offs in measurability, customer comprehension, value alignment, and vendor risk.

At the infrastructure end of the spectrum sit **consumption metrics**: tokens, API calls, compute minutes, storage gigabytes. These metrics are precise, easy to meter, and directly tied to the vendor's cost of production. The vendor knows exactly what each unit costs to deliver, which makes margin management straightforward. The problem is that consumption metrics are meaningful to engineers and meaningless to everyone else. A VP of Customer Support does not think in tokens. A Chief Legal Officer does not budget in API calls. Consumption metrics work for technical buyer personas — developers, data engineers, ML engineers — who understand the underlying infrastructure and can translate tokens into business value. They fail for business buyer personas who want to know what the product does, not how it works.

In the middle of the spectrum sit **workflow metrics**: documents processed, analyses completed, reports generated, conversations handled, code reviews performed. These metrics describe units of work that the customer recognizes and values. A customer who processes contracts understands "per contract." A customer who generates reports understands "per report." Workflow metrics are easier for customers to forecast because they correlate with business activity: if you process 500 contracts per month, you can predict your AI bill. They are harder for the vendor to manage because the cost of producing each workflow unit varies with complexity. A ten-page contract and a two-hundred-page contract are both "one document analyzed," but the second costs twenty times more to process.

At the outcome end of the spectrum sit **outcome metrics**: tickets resolved, leads qualified, fraud cases prevented, compliance issues detected. These metrics describe results that the customer's business cares about. They represent the highest possible alignment between price and value because the customer pays only when the product delivers a measurable business result. They also represent the highest risk for the vendor because the vendor absorbs the cost of every attempt that does not produce a billable outcome, as covered in the previous two subchapters.

## The Trade-Off Matrix

Each position on the spectrum involves a trade-off between four dimensions. Understanding these trade-offs is the framework for choosing the right metric for your product.

The first dimension is **customer comprehension**. How easily can the customer understand and forecast their costs? Consumption metrics score lowest — most business buyers cannot estimate token usage. Workflow metrics score in the middle — buyers can estimate how many documents or analyses they need, but complexity variance introduces uncertainty. Outcome metrics score highest — buyers know how many tickets they receive or how many leads they need. Comprehension drives sales velocity. The easier the metric is to understand, the faster deals close.

The second dimension is **value alignment**. How closely does the charge metric track the value the customer receives? Consumption metrics score lowest — tokens consumed have no inherent relationship to value delivered. A hallucinated response consumes the same tokens as a perfect one. Workflow metrics score in the middle — a processed document has some value, but the value varies with the quality and usefulness of the analysis. Outcome metrics score highest — a resolved ticket or a qualified lead is value by definition. Higher value alignment justifies higher prices because the customer can see exactly what they are paying for.

The third dimension is **margin predictability**. How well can the vendor predict and manage margins? Consumption metrics score highest — every token costs a known amount, and the vendor's margin on each unit is stable and calculable. Workflow metrics score in the middle — cost per workflow unit varies with complexity, but the variance is bounded and manageable with routing and tiering. Outcome metrics score lowest — the vendor absorbs the cost of failed attempts, and the cost per successful outcome depends on the resolution rate, which can fluctuate with product changes, customer behavior, and the complexity distribution of incoming work.

The fourth dimension is **competitive defensibility**. How hard is it for a competitor to undercut you? Consumption metrics are the least defensible — any competitor with access to cheaper models or more efficient infrastructure can offer lower per-token rates, and the customer has no reason to stay because one token is the same as another. Workflow metrics are moderately defensible — quality differences in processing matter, but competitors can still compete on price. Outcome metrics are the most defensible — the competitor has to match your resolution rate, your outcome quality, and your pricing simultaneously. An outcome-based vendor with a sixty-five percent resolution rate and a $0.99 price is hard to undercut because the competitor needs to match both the economics and the effectiveness.

## Choosing by Buyer Persona

The charge metric should match the buyer, not the product. This principle sounds obvious, but most teams violate it because they choose the metric that matches their internal architecture rather than their customer's mental model.

If your primary buyer is a **technical persona** — a developer, an ML engineer, a data scientist — consumption metrics can work. These buyers understand tokens, API calls, and compute resources. They evaluate products on technical merit and price efficiency. They build their own ROI models. GitHub Copilot for developers, Anthropic's Claude API for developers, and OpenAI's API for developers all use consumption metrics successfully because the buyer thinks in those units.

If your primary buyer is a **functional leader** — a VP of Customer Support, a Head of Legal Operations, a Director of Sales Development — workflow or outcome metrics are almost always correct. These buyers do not think in tokens. They think in tickets, contracts, leads, and reports. They evaluate products on business impact and total cost of ownership. They need pricing they can put in a budget request without an engineering team to translate. Intercom prices per resolution. Harvey, the legal AI platform, prices per seat for its core platform but is exploring workflow metrics for specific use cases. The charge metric matches the buyer's vocabulary.

If your buyer is a **C-level executive** — a CFO, a COO, a CTO making a company-wide platform decision — outcome metrics are the most compelling because executives think in business results. But executives also need predictability, which is why hybrid models with outcome components and platform fee floors are the typical structure for enterprise deals. The executive approves the platform fee as a predictable line item and accepts the outcome-based variable component as an investment with measurable, auditable returns.

## The Credit Abstraction Layer

A growing number of AI companies in 2026 have adopted **credits** as an abstraction layer that sits between the customer and the underlying consumption metrics. Instead of billing per token or per API call, the company defines a credit as a standardized unit of work. Different operations consume different numbers of credits: a simple query might cost one credit, a complex analysis might cost ten credits, and a multi-step agent workflow might cost twenty-five credits. The customer buys credits and spends them as they use the product.

Credits solve the comprehension problem of consumption metrics without fully committing to workflow or outcome metrics. The customer does not need to understand tokens — they understand credits. Credits are also flexible. As the vendor adds new features with different cost profiles, they assign credit costs to each feature without changing the pricing model. A new document summarization capability that costs the vendor more to produce can be assigned a higher credit cost without renegotiating every customer contract.

But credits have a trap. As Bessemer's research notes, credits work best as a temporary architecture while teams gather usage data, refine telemetry, and learn what customers actually value. The danger is that credits become permanent, creating a layer of indirection that obscures the relationship between price and value. A customer who has spent 10,000 credits does not know whether they got 10,000 credits' worth of value. They know they spent 10,000 credits. The credit itself has no inherent meaning — it is an arbitrary unit defined by the vendor. Over time, customers start asking: "What is a credit actually worth to me?" If the vendor cannot answer that question clearly, the abstraction that was supposed to simplify pricing has instead obscured it.

The strategic use of credits is as a transitional metric. Launch with credits while you gather usage data. Analyze which workflows and outcomes customers value most. Then migrate to a workflow or outcome metric that directly maps to the value the customer recognizes. Credits buy time. They should not buy permanent residency in your pricing model.

## Five Diagnostic Questions for Choosing Your Charge Metric

If you are designing a pricing model from scratch — or questioning whether your current charge metric is right — run through these five questions. The answers will point you toward the correct position on the spectrum.

First question: can your customer estimate their monthly usage of this metric before they buy? If the customer can say "we process about 2,000 contracts per month" or "we handle about 50,000 support tickets per month," the metric is forecastable and the customer will feel comfortable signing a variable-rate contract. If the customer has no idea how many tokens or API calls they will consume, the metric creates procurement friction that slows or kills deals.

Second question: does this metric appear in the customer's existing budget or reporting? A customer support leader has a budget line for "cost per ticket." A legal department has a budget line for "outside counsel spend per matter." If your charge metric maps to an existing budget category, the customer can approve spending without creating a new line item. If your metric requires the customer to create a new budget category called "AI credits" or "token consumption," you have added a procurement step that did not need to exist.

Third question: can a non-technical executive understand the ROI in one sentence? "We pay $0.99 per resolved ticket instead of $12 per human-resolved ticket" passes this test. "We pay $0.002 per thousand input tokens and $0.008 per thousand output tokens, and an average query consumes 3,200 input tokens and 650 output tokens, so the cost per query is roughly $0.012, and we estimate 400,000 queries per month, so the monthly cost is approximately $4,800" does not pass this test. The first metric sells itself. The second requires a spreadsheet and an explainer document.

Fourth question: does the metric stay stable as you improve the product? If you optimize your models to use fewer tokens per query, a per-token metric means your revenue drops as your product improves — you are penalized for efficiency. If you charge per document or per resolution, your revenue stays constant as you improve efficiency, and the cost reduction flows to your margin. Metrics that move in the wrong direction when the product improves are structurally hostile to engineering investment.

Fifth question: does the metric create the customer behavior you want? Per-token pricing discourages usage because every interaction costs money. Per-seat pricing encourages shelfware because the cost is fixed regardless of usage. Per-resolution pricing encourages the customer to route more issues to the AI, because each resolution saves them money compared to the human alternative. The charge metric is an incentive system. It shapes how customers interact with your product. Choose the metric that encourages the behavior that makes both you and the customer successful.

## The Charge Metric Migration Problem

Changing your charge metric after launch is one of the most disruptive business decisions you can make. Every existing customer contract, every sales playbook, every competitive positioning document, every financial forecast is built on the current metric. A migration from per-token to per-document, or from per-credit to per-resolution, requires renegotiating or restructuring every existing customer agreement, retraining the sales team on a new value narrative, rebuilding the billing system to meter and invoice on the new unit, and resetting customer expectations about what they pay and why.

This disruption is why the charge metric decision matters so much at the outset. Get it right and your pricing model scales with your business for years. Get it wrong and you face a painful, customer-disruptive migration that can take six to twelve months and risks churn during the transition.

If you must migrate, the least disruptive approach is to run parallel pricing for a transition period. Keep the old metric active for existing customers. Introduce the new metric for new customers. At each existing customer's renewal, offer a migration to the new metric with pricing that is economically equivalent or slightly favorable. This minimizes disruption and gives existing customers a natural transition point. The risk is that you operate two billing systems simultaneously, which doubles the operational complexity of pricing, billing, and revenue recognition. But that operational complexity is far less costly than the customer churn that a forced, abrupt migration would trigger.

Salesforce's migration from $2 per conversation to $0.10 per action Flex Credits illustrates both the pain and the pragmatism. The pivot was necessary because the original metric was wrong — "conversation" did not align with customer value. But the migration required Salesforce to create entirely new packaging, force existing customers to choose between the old and new models (they cannot run both in the same organization), and retrain its massive sales force on a fundamentally different pricing narrative. The cost of that migration — in engineering, in sales productivity, in customer confusion — is a tax Salesforce will be paying throughout 2026. The lesson is not that migration is impossible. It is that choosing the right metric at the outset saves you from paying that tax at all.

## The Strategic Statement

Bessemer captures the meta-principle cleanly: your charge metric is a strategic statement, not just a billing decision. It tells the market what kind of company you are. A company that charges per token is an infrastructure company. A company that charges per document is a workflow company. A company that charges per resolution is an outcomes company. Each identity attracts a different customer, supports a different sales motion, and justifies a different valuation multiple.

Infrastructure companies are valued like cloud services: on revenue multiples with heavy emphasis on gross margin and retention. Workflow companies are valued like SaaS businesses: on ARR multiples with emphasis on growth rate and net dollar retention. Outcome companies are valued like high-value vertical SaaS: on premium multiples with emphasis on customer ROI and switching costs. The charge metric you choose positions your company on this spectrum, and the position influences how investors value you, how acquirers evaluate you, and how the market categorizes you.

Choose the metric that matches where you want to be on the spectrum — not where your infrastructure makes it easy to be. If you are building a workflow product, charge for workflows even though tokens are easier to meter. If you are building an outcome product, charge for outcomes even though workflows are easier to define. The engineering work to meter the right metric is a one-time investment. The strategic positioning that the metric creates compounds for the life of the business.

Once you have chosen the right charge metric, the next question is the one that keeps every pricing leader awake: what number do you actually put next to it?
